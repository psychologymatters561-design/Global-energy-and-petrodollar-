1859 — 1914
The Birth of Oil Power
From a 69-foot hole in Pennsylvania to the Royal Navy's fuel tanks — how oil went from curiosity to the planet's most strategic molecule in 55 years.
AUGUST 27, 1859
Edwin Drake strikes oil at Titusville, Pennsylvania. 69-foot well. Initial flow: 25 barrels/day. Price: $20/barrel. Within months: 2,000 wells drilled across Pennsylvania. Price collapses to $0.10/barrel. The first oil glut — a pattern that never ends.
1870
John D. Rockefeller founds Standard Oil of Ohio. His insight: whoever controls the pipelines and refineries controls oil — not whoever pumps it. Within 12 years, Standard controls 91% of US refining capacity and 79% of oil sales.
1879 — 1882
The Baku oil rush. Azerbaijan (then Russian Empire) surpasses Pennsylvania in production. Nobel Brothers, Rothschilds, and Russian capital flood in. For the first time: oil becomes a geopolitical instrument between European powers.
1882 — 1900
Rockefeller's Standard Oil Trust controls pricing, pipelines, and distribution across the US. He undercuts competitors, acquires their refineries, then raises prices. The original playbook for monopoly capitalism — executed in oil.
1908
Anglo-Persian Oil Company (APOC) strikes oil in Masjid Suleiman, Persia. William Knox D'Arcy's concession covers 500,000 square miles. Britain doesn't just find oil — it finds an empire built on oil.
1911
Standard Oil broken up by Supreme Court into 34 companies — which would slowly reconsolidate into today's Exxon, Mobil, Chevron, Amoco, and others. The monopoly was defeated legally. The companies simply became global instead of domestic.
1914
Churchill converts the Royal Navy from coal to oil. The British government buys 51% of Anglo-Persian Oil Company. For the first time in history, a major power's military is structurally dependent on foreign oil. The geopolitics of energy warfare begins here.

1914 — 1945
Oil Becomes War Fuel
Two world wars. Both substantially determined by who had oil and who didn't. The nations that ran out, lost.
Strategic Thesis: World War II was substantially a resource war. Germany needed Caucasus oil. Japan needed Dutch East Indies oil. Both gambled their entire military strategy on seizing foreign oil fields. Both failed. Both lost the war as a direct consequence.
WWI — Oil's First Military Decisive Role

Britain: domestic oil production near zero, but controlled Persian and Mesopotamian oil through Anglo-Persian and colonial administration. French military ran on oil shipped from Standard Oil. German military ran on Romanian Ploiești oil and coal-to-liquid conversion — a desperate 2nd-best solution.

The decisive factor: German submarines could not stop British oil supply lines from Persia. Allied mechanized superiority — tanks, planes, motorized artillery — was ultimately a function of oil access.

Lord Curzon, 1918: "The Allied cause floated to victory on a wave of oil."

The Cartel Agreements — 1928

Red Line Agreement (1928): Drew a literal red line around the former Ottoman Empire. The IPC consortium (British, French, American, Dutch companies) agreed that none would pursue oil independently within that zone. Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and the Gulf — all divided on paper before a drop was found.

Achnacarry "As Is" Agreement (1928): Seven Sisters meet at Achnacarry Castle, Scotland. Agree to freeze global market shares, accept each other's existing positions, pool resources, and fix prices to prevent "destructive competition." This is the most consequential price-fixing agreement in history — for a commodity that every nation needed.

WWII — The Oil Campaigns

Germany's oil problem: Germany produced 11.6 million tons of oil equivalent domestically in 1938 — enough for peacetime. War consumed 44+ million tons/year. Solution: seize Caucasus oilfields (Baku, Grozny, Maikop). Operation Blue / Case Blue (1942) — the entire summer offensive aimed at Caucasus oil. Paulus's 6th Army at Stalingrad was protecting the flank of the Caucasus drive. The German army died at Stalingrad protecting an oil objective they never reached.

Japan's oil problem: Japan imported 80% of its oil from the United States. In 1941, FDR imposed a full oil embargo. Japan had 18 months of strategic reserves. The math was simple: attack the Dutch East Indies oil fields (Sumatra, Borneo, Java) — or cease to function as a military power. Pearl Harbor was designed to destroy the US Pacific Fleet and buy time to secure Southeast Asian oil. The entire Pacific War theater was an oil-access war.

1945 — 1970
The American Century of Oil
The Seven Sisters ran a complete monopoly — upstream, midstream, downstream. They set the price. They chose who sold. They chose who bought. One CIA operation restored that order when it was threatened.

The posted price system was the Seven Sisters' masterpiece of economic fiction. The Sisters would publish an official "posted price" for crude — but then sell to their own subsidiaries (which they owned) at a discount to that price. The producing countries received royalties calculated on the posted price, not the real transaction price. Result: every barrel produced paid less to the nation that owned the oil than the paper said.

The 50/50 profit-sharing breakthrough came first in Venezuela (1948), then in Saudi Arabia (1950) — when Aramco signed a 50/50 deal rather than face nationalization. This sounds fair. It wasn't. The companies still set the posted price. 50% of a manipulated number is still a manipulated number.

Operation TPAJAX — Iran 1953 — Documented CIA Action
Context: Mohammad Mossadegh, elected Prime Minister of Iran, nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company (AIOC) in 1951. The British responded with a naval blockade and global embargo of Iranian oil. The US initially was neutral — then changed position when Britain framed it as a communist threat.

Operation Ajax (US name) / Operation Boot (British name): CIA and MI6 funded street riots, paid military officers, organized a coup. Mossadegh was arrested August 19, 1953. The Shah — Mohammed Reza Pahlavi — was restored. A new oil consortium was formed: 40% US companies, 40% British, 14% Dutch (Shell), 6% French. The nationalization was undone.

What this tells you: When a producing nation threatened to keep its own oil revenues, it was removed. The operation was declassified in 2013. This is documented history, not speculation.

1954 — 1975
The Vietnam War — America's Oil Gambit in Asia
The Vietnam War was not simply a Cold War confrontation. It was a resource war to keep Southeast Asia's oil potential inside the dollar orbit — and it ended by destroying the dollar's gold foundation, forcing America to invent the petrodollar system to survive.
The Hidden Ledger: The Vietnam War cost $843 billion (2023 USD). US gold reserves fell from $22 billion to under $10 billion as allies converted surplus dollars into gold. By 1971, Nixon had no choice: close the gold window or watch Fort Knox drain empty. The war that destroyed Vietnam also destroyed the Bretton Woods system — and gave birth to the petrodollar.
Why Southeast Asia — The Oil Calculus

The "Domino Theory" presented Vietnam as a chess piece in the ideological Cold War. The strategic reality was deeper: Southeast Asia sits above enormous hydrocarbon potential. The South China Sea basin — bordered by Vietnam, Indonesia, the Philippines, and Malaysia — was understood by US geological surveys to contain significant offshore oil and gas.

Indonesia alone held proven reserves developed by Caltex (a Chevron-Texaco joint venture) since the 1930s. A communist-aligned Southeast Asia meant those reserves — and the Strait of Malacca, through which all regional trade moved — would exit the dollar system entirely.

Vietnam's oil: Mobil Oil's offshore surveys in 1974–75, conducted just as Saigon fell, identified the Bach Ho ("White Tiger") field — one of the region's most productive deposits. Had the war been "won," American oil companies would have developed it. Instead it became Vietnam's state oil company crown asset, producing over 100 million barrels at peak. The oil was always there. The war was partly about who would control it.

Gulf of Tonkin — Documented Deception, August 1964

August 2, 1964: USS Maddox exchanged fire with North Vietnamese torpedo boats. This attack was real.

August 4, 1964: A second reported attack on USS Maddox and USS Turner Joy. This attack was fabricated. NSA historian Robert Hanyok's 2001 classified study — declassified in 2005 — concluded that signals intelligence had been deliberately falsified to support a second attack that never occurred. Defense Secretary McNamara later admitted this to documentarians before his death.

The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution (August 7, 1964) gave President Johnson unlimited authority to use military force in Southeast Asia without a formal declaration of war. This single document, built on a manufactured incident, authorized the commitment of 540,000 US troops, $843 billion in expenditure, and the chain of events that broke Bretton Woods.

The same waters where the fabricated incident was staged sit above oil-bearing geology. The same Strait of Malacca that US warships patrolled carries 16 mb/d of oil today.

Nixon Shock — August 15, 1971: Vietnam Breaks Bretton Woods

By 1971, the Vietnam War had consumed the dollar's credibility. The US ran persistent trade deficits. Foreign governments — led by France under de Gaulle — systematically converted dollar reserves into gold at the guaranteed $35/oz rate, draining Fort Knox from $22 billion to under $10 billion.

August 15, 1971: Nixon announces the "temporary" suspension of dollar-gold convertibility. The Bretton Woods system — constructed in 1944 to anchor the world monetary order — is unilaterally ended by the country that built it.

Every nation's currency reserve, held in dollars as the gold equivalent, is suddenly backed by nothing but American political will. Oil — priced in dollars — is now priced in a floating fiat currency. The stability of the global oil trade requires a new anchor.

Kissinger's insight: peg the dollar to oil instead of gold. The petrodollar deal was not an innovation. It was a repair job for a system broken by the Vietnam War.

INDONESIA 1965 — The Coup That Saved Southeast Asia's Oil — Documented
Context: President Sukarno had moved Indonesia toward the Non-Aligned Movement, allied with the PKI (Indonesian Communist Party — the world's third largest), and was nationalizing foreign oil operations including Dutch Shell. Indonesia held vast reserves across Sumatra, Borneo, and Java — the same fields Japan fought WWII to control.

September 30 / October 1, 1965: A coup attempt blamed on the PKI. General Suharto seized power and conducted a nationwide purge. Between 500,000 and 1,000,000 people were killed — primarily alleged communists and ethnic Chinese. The CIA provided lists of PKI members to Indonesian military units. This was confirmed by CIA official Robert Martens (admitted to AP journalist Kathy Kadane, 1990) and by former US Ambassador Marshall Green in his 1990 memoir.

What followed within months: US oil companies received preferential access to Indonesian oil fields immediately under Suharto. Caltex (Chevron-Texaco), Continental Oil, and others signed Production Sharing Contracts that had been refused under Sukarno. Indonesia joined OPEC in 1962 but became a compliant supplier. The world's fourth most populous nation — sitting astride the Malacca Strait — remained within the dollar orbit.

Sources: Declassified State Department cables (2017 release), Kathy Kadane AP investigation (1990), NSA archive, US Embassy Jakarta cables, Robert Martens' on-record admissions.

1947 — 1991
The Cold War — Oil's Invisible Battlefield
Two nuclear superpowers. Forty-four years of confrontation. The Cold War's decisive battles were not fought with missiles — they were fought with oil production quotas, pipeline politics, and petrodollar flows. The nation that understood this last, lost the war.
The Core Thesis: The Soviet Union was an oil empire disguised as an ideological movement. When oil prices were high (1973–1985), it could fund its military, its client states, and its welfare system simultaneously. When oil prices were engineered to crash in 1986 — by Washington coordinating with Riyadh — the mathematics of Soviet empire became impossible. No empire in history has collapsed faster once its primary revenue source was cut by 80%.
1947 — The Opening Gambit
Truman Doctrine, March 12, 1947: The US would support "free peoples resisting subjugation." Immediate context: Turkey and Greece under Soviet pressure — both sitting on oil transit routes. The Marshall Plan ($13B, 1948) rebuilt Western Europe with an implicit purpose: prevent oil-dependent European economies from drifting into the Soviet orbit by economic desperation. Energy dependency was the vulnerability from the first day of the Cold War.
1950 — The Foundational Oil Asymmetry
In 1950, the US produced 52% of global oil. The Soviet Union produced approximately 7%. This asymmetry was the Cold War's bedrock. America could fuel its military, its allies, and the global economy simultaneously. The USSR could not. Soviet grand strategy therefore centered on denying US access to others' oil — not out-producing it. Every proxy war, every coup, every arms shipment to the Middle East was about oil access.
1953–1960 — Middle East as Battleground
The 1953 Iran coup (CIA/MI6, Operation Ajax) was framed as preventing Soviet influence — correct. The Suez Crisis (1956): Nasser's Egypt, armed with Soviet weapons, nationalized the Suez Canal. The US forced Britain, France, and Israel to withdraw — asserting American primacy in Middle East oil management. Arab nationalism became the Cold War's central resource problem: neither superpower could control it directly. Both competed for its favor with weapons and money.
1961–1970 — The Soviet Oil Boom Changes Everything
The discovery of the West Siberian oil basin — particularly the Samotlor supergiant field (1961, 13.6 billion barrels) — transformed the Soviet economy. By the early 1970s, the USSR had become the world's largest oil producer. Peak production: 12.01 mb/d (1987). Oil and gas exports became the USSR's primary hard currency earner — 60–80% of all foreign exchange revenues. This structural dependency on oil revenue was the vulnerability Reagan's team would later exploit with devastating precision.
1973–1979 — The Windfall That Masked the Rot
The 1973 oil crisis was a Soviet windfall. As a major oil exporter, every dollar increase in oil price was pure gain. Soviet export revenues soared from approximately $8B to $32B annually. This funded a global empire of client states: Cuba (subsidized oil below market), Vietnam (post-war reconstruction), Angola (MPLA support), Ethiopia, South Yemen, Nicaragua, and ultimately Afghanistan. The petrodollar era that Kissinger built to save American hegemony simultaneously financed Soviet global projection. Oil's price rise armed both sides.
1979 — Afghanistan: The Trap Is Set
Soviet invasion of Afghanistan: December 24–27, 1979. Strategic rationale: protect a Soviet-aligned government; deny the US a potential base threatening Central Asian oil infrastructure; prevent encirclement of Siberian fields. Zbigniew Brzezinski's counter-strategy: "We now have the opportunity of giving to the USSR its Vietnam War." CIA Operation Cyclone began funding Mujahideen resistance — from $20M/year initially to $630M/year by 1987, the largest CIA covert operation in history. The trap was sprung.
1985 — The Reagan-Fahd Agreement: The Killing Blow
CIA Director William Casey visited Riyadh multiple times in 1984–85. The arrangement: Saudi Arabia would abandon OPEC production quotas and flood the market. Production rose from approximately 2 mb/d to 9 mb/d. Brent crude crashed from $30 to below $10/barrel by early 1986 — a 67% collapse in months. Soviet hard currency earnings fell from approximately $32 billion to under $6 billion annually. The Afghan war was costing $8B/year. The Soviet budget deficit exploded to 9% of GDP. The empire was economically unviable.
1989–1991 — The Collapse
Gorbachev's Glasnost and Perestroika were attempts to reform a system broken simultaneously by: oil revenue collapse, military overspending, and Afghan war attrition. Berlin Wall fell November 9, 1989. The USSR dissolved December 25, 1991. The Cold War ended not on a battlefield but at a commodity trading desk, where the oil price sustaining the Soviet empire was sent into freefall by a Saudi production decision coordinated in Washington.

The Price Mechanism — London Sets the Price
The price of a barrel of Saudi crude is set, every day, during a 30-minute window in London. The Saudis are not in that room.
How Brent Price Is Actually Set

Platts/S&P Global Market Assessment: Every business day, 4:00 PM – 4:30 PM London time, Platts assesses bids and offers in the North Sea crude market — Brent, Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk, Troll (BFOET). The assessed price becomes "Dated Brent" — the global reference price.

Approximately 65-70% of globally traded crude is priced as a differential to Dated Brent. Saudi Arabia's official selling price to Asia is "Dated Brent +/- X." Iraqi crude to Europe is "Dated Brent - Y." The entire global oil price structure rests on what a few traders in London say during a 30-minute window.

North Sea physical production is now less than 1 mb/d — a tiny fraction of global supply. Its price sets the reference for 80 mb/d of traded oil. This structural asymmetry is one of the most consequential facts in global energy finance.

Paper vs Physical — Scale Mismatch

Global oil consumption: ~106 mb/d = approximately 38.7 billion barrels/year of physical oil.

ICE Brent futures open interest regularly represents 400-600 million barrels of notional exposure — changing hands daily. Annual futures volume: approximately 400-600 billion barrels equivalent — 10-15x the physical market.

Goldman Sachs's 2005 "super-spike" prediction (oil to $100+) was widely credited with accelerating the 2007-2008 price surge. A bank research note moved the price of a commodity that powers the global economy. The paper market drives the physical price — not the other way around.
